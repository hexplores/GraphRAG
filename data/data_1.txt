Title: The History of Coffee

Coffee originated in Ethiopia, where legend says a goat herder named Kaldi first discovered the energizing effects of coffee beans. By the 15th century, coffee was cultivated in Yemen and spread across the Middle East.

Coffee houses, known as "qahveh khaneh," became centers for social activity and political discussion. By the 17th century, coffee had reached Europe and eventually the Americas.

Today, coffee is one of the most traded commodities in the world, with Brazil being the largest producer.

Regional styles vary widely. In Italy, espresso culture emphasizes short, concentrated shots. In Turkey, coffee is brewed unfiltered and served with grounds. In Vietnam, robusta beans are often paired with sweetened condensed milk.

Brewing methods include drip, French press, pour-over, AeroPress, and cold brew. Each method changes extraction time, body, and acidity.

Coffee processing shapes flavor. Washed coffees tend to be clean and bright, while natural processed coffees are fruitier and heavier-bodied. Honey process falls in between.

Storage and freshness matter. Whole beans keep better than pre-ground coffee. Airtight containers and cool, dark storage help preserve aromas.

Roast levels range from light to dark. Light roasts retain more acidity and origin flavors, while dark roasts emphasize caramelized, smoky notes.

Grind size affects extraction. Fine grinds extract quickly for espresso, while coarse grinds suit French press and cold brew.

Water quality matters. Balanced minerals improve extraction; overly soft or hard water can flatten flavors.
